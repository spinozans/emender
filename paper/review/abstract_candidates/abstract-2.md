A language model whose recurrence is nonlinear in time, meaning the state passes through a nonlinearity at every step and so forecloses the parallel scan that linear recurrences rely on for throughput, reaches below one bit per byte on The Pile using a single workstation-class GPU. Throughput is taken from the width axis: hundreds of small recurrent programs per token, each running its time loop serially. On held-out bits per byte, the measurement that decides the comparison, two such nonlinear-in-time models, one delta-correcting and one raw-write, together with a linear-recurrent baseline form a statistical tie; bulk language-model loss does not tell them apart. Two distinct axes do. Linearity is a question of computability: the linear recurrence cannot track non-solvable group state as length grows, a converged ceiling and not undertraining. The write rule is a question of efficiency: at matched budget the delta-correcting update generalizes far past its training length where the raw-write update under-reaches. A Lean 4 trusted core proves the delta update reaches a strictly larger one-step function class at matched compute.
