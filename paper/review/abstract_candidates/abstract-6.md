A recurrent language model whose state passes through a nonlinearity at every step, which forecloses the parallel scan linear recurrences use for throughput, reaches below one bit per byte on The Pile on a single workstation-class GPU. Throughput comes from width, not time: hundreds of small recurrent programs run per token, each iterating its time loop serially. The measurement is held-out bits per byte. There, two such nonlinear models and a linear-recurrent baseline tie, and bulk language-model loss does not tell them apart. Two axes do. Computability: the linear recurrence cannot track non-solvable group state as length grows, a converged ceiling, not undertraining. Learning efficiency: between two nonlinear writes at matched budget, the delta-correcting update length-generalizes the non-solvable task far past its training length while raw-write under-reaches. A Lean 4 trusted core proves the second gap at its root: delta correction reaches a strictly larger one-step function class than raw-write at matched compute.
